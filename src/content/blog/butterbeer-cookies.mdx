<ProblemCard>
<p>My family just got sorted into four different Hogwarts houses. All four of us matched exactly the ones we'd already picked for ourselves. I wanted to mark the moment with something. The only problem was I had no idea what, or how.</p>
</ProblemCard>

Over winter break, my family went to Universal Studios Florida. It was a late Christmas gift and birthday celebration for my husband and son, and the timing turned out to be perfect. Universal had just opened Epic Universe, with a brand new Harry Potter world and a new ride. We are a Harry Potter household. We were ready.

It was our first time staying on site, which meant hotel boats directly to the parks every morning. Small thing, genuinely delightful. We stayed at Portofino Bay, which sounds fancier than it felt. It felt easy and unhurried, which is the best way a vacation can feel.

When we arrived, we needed lanyards for our park passes. My daughter went straight for Gryffindor. My son picked Ravenclaw. My husband, without hesitation, chose Slytherin. I got the one with all four houses on it because I didn't want to commit. I didn't know where I belonged yet.

<Image src={hogwartsExpress} alt="Kid looking at the Hogwarts Express at Universal Studios" width={500} />

We spent a lot of time in the new Harry Potter world. The kids got new wands. We were one of the first in line for the ride. And we all had butterbeer, frozen, with that warm cream poured over the top, which melts down through the cold and produces something that is genuinely difficult to describe if you haven't had it. My daughter drank hers without the cream, which I still cannot explain, but she loved it anyway.

When we got home, I took the official sorting quiz on the Wizarding World site. I matched into Hufflepuff.

Hufflepuff was the only house that hadn't been claimed by anyone else in my family. I told my husband and he immediately took the quiz too. He placed into Slytherin, exactly the house he'd always felt was his. The next day I had the kids take it. My daughter, who had chosen the Gryffindor lanyard on day one, matched into Gryffindor. My son, who had picked Ravenclaw, matched into Ravenclaw.

All four of us. Four different houses. All of us exactly where we'd already put ourselves (well, where I finally realized I belonged).

I found these house glasses online, one for each of us in our house colors, and decided to give them as a little post-trip gift. But I wanted a sweet treat to go with them.

<Image src={houseGlasses} alt="Four Hogwarts house glasses in yellow, red, blue, and green, one for each member of the family" width={500} />

I kept thinking about the butterbeer.

When I went looking for butterbeer recipes online, everything I found was fudge. Or butterscotch bark. Things that were very sweet and very dense and tasted nothing like what I remembered from the park. What I was chasing wasn't butterscotch flavor. It was that moment when the warm cream hits the frozen drink and everything changes. That contrast. That unexpected softness.

I didn't know how to make that into a dessert. But I knew where I could go for an answer.

A stuffed cookie. Buttery dough with a cream filling that would soften when warmed, the same way the cream runs through the butterbeer at the park. The more I thought about it, the more right it felt.

I got the ingredients and ran into my first problem almost immediately.

The recipe called for browning the butter.

I had never browned butter on purpose before. I've browned it by accident plenty of times. That's just what happens when you're trying to make eggs and something else gets your attention. But doing it deliberately, correctly, knowing when to stop? I had no idea what I was looking for.

Every resource said the same thing: it's ready when it smells nutty.

Well...I'm allergic to nuts. I have no clear sense of what nutty smells like.

So I took a picture and asked.

From that point I just kept documenting as I went. Not because I was planning to share it, just because it was working and I didn't want to mess it up. Showing AI what I was doing felt like having a second set of eyes in the kitchen.

<Image src={cookiesFinished} alt="Finished butterbeer stuffed cookies on a plate, one broken open to show the cream filling inside" width={500} />

The cream came out well. Light, not too sweet, with just enough cream cheese to hold it together. My husband loved them. I even made one for my daughter without the filling.

They were good, but not exactly what I wanted. So I went back to the source.

<ScienceNote label="why taste takes you somewhere">
Sensory memory, taste, texture, smell, is processed through the limbic system, the same brain region involved in emotion and memory consolidation. This is why a specific flavor can take you somewhere instantly in a way that a photograph often can't. For ADHD brains, which frequently struggle with prospective memory, sensory anchors can be unusually powerful. The butterbeer wasn't just a flavor to recreate. It was a feeling from a specific moment I wanted to bring home.
</ScienceNote>

<WhatWorked>
<ul>
<li>When you can't find what you're looking for online, describe the feeling instead of the thing. "I want to recreate the moment when warm cream hits cold butterbeer" got somewhere. "Butterbeer recipe" did not.</li>
<li>AI doesn't get attached to the first idea. It suggested custard before we landed on the cookie. Your instinct plus its suggestions gets you somewhere neither of you would have landed alone.</li>
<li>If a recipe uses sensory cues you can't access, take a photo and ask. This is an underrated use of vision-capable AI and it saved the whole batch.</li>
<li>Document as you cook, not for social media, for yourself. Showing AI your progress in real time keeps you accountable to the plan.</li>
<li>Feedback is part of the process, not a sign the process failed. Version 1 gave us exactly what we needed to build Version 2.</li>
</ul>
</WhatWorked>
